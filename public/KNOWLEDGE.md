## 1) Reliability, Resilience & Operations

- **SLA (Service Level Agreement)**  
  *What it is:* Contracted service target (availability, response times).  
  *Use it:* Board‑level assurance, regulatory narratives, vendor governance.

- **SLO (Service Level Objective)**  
  *What it is:* Internal target that supports/feeds SLA.  
  *Use it:* Engineering commitments and resilience objectives.

- **SLI (Service Level Indicator)**  
  *What it is:* Actual measurement (e.g., uptime %, p95 latency).  
  *Use it:* Evidence to validate SLO/SLA attainment.

- **MTTR (Mean Time to Recovery/Restore/Resolve)**  
  *What it is:* Average time to recover from incidents.  
  *Use it:* Demonstrate operational maturity (CI/CD, rollback, reliability).

- **MTTD (Mean Time to Detect)**  
  *What it is:* Time to detect an incident.  
  *Use it:* Risk control, monitoring maturity.

- **MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures)**  
  *What it is:* Average time between failures.  
  *Use it:* System stability over time.

- **RTO (Recovery Time Objective)**  
  *What it is:* Max allowable downtime.  
  *Use it:* Business continuity commitments.

- **RPO (Recovery Point Objective)**  
  *What it is:* Max allowable data loss window.  
  *Use it:* Risk and data protection policy alignment.

- **P0 / P1 / P2 (Incident Priority Levels)**  
  *What it is:* Severity tiers driving response and escalation.

  *What it is:* Forum for risk‑aware change approvals.

## 12) Technical Prep — Kafka & Event‑Driven Systems at 8B+ Daily Transactions

### A) Framing the Problem (how to speak about scale)
- **Volume:** 8B/day ≈ 92,500 events/sec on average; design for peak bursts (often 5–10x).  
- **Latency:** distinguish *end‑to‑end* latency vs. *broker* latency.  
- **Criticality:** trading systems require deterministic ordering, resilience, and auditability.

At scale, “8B/day” is not the design target — *peak* and *burst* conditions are. You should talk about how you size for worst‑case spikes (e.g., market events), not just average throughput. Emphasize that your architecture decisions are driven by **peak TPS**, **tail latency**, and **business impact** (P&L, risk exposure, and missed executions).

Be explicit about **service boundaries**: which events must be strongly ordered vs. those that can be eventually consistent. This allows you to argue for partitioning and aggregation strategies that preserve ordering only where the business demands it (e.g., per account, per portfolio, per trading book).

Frame latency in terms executives understand: *“From market data arrival to trade decision to execution confirmation.”* That end‑to‑end pipeline matters more than any single component’s latency, and it provides a strong narrative for prioritizing instrumentation, buffering, and throughput tuning.

Finally, emphasize **auditability** and **traceability**. Financial systems are not only about speed; they also require full lineage, replay, and evidence for risk, compliance, and post‑trade analysis. Event logs are a business asset.

### B) Core Architecture Principles
- **Partition for scale:** choose partition key that preserves ordering where needed (e.g., portfolio ID, account ID).  
- **Replication factor (RF):** typically 3 for production; balance durability with cost.  
- **Rack awareness:** protect against AZ failures.  
- **Backpressure:** consumer lag alerting + auto‑scaling consumers.

Partitioning is the single most important decision for Kafka scalability. You should explain how you choose keys based on **business ordering constraints** and **hot‑key avoidance**. A great interview answer shows you can trade off strict ordering for throughput where appropriate.

Replication is a resilience decision, not just a checkbox. Discuss how RF and ISR (in‑sync replicas) targets are balanced with write latency, and how you ensure brokers are not a single point of failure in an AZ. Mention **rack awareness** and controlled broker placement.

Backpressure is how you keep the system stable under bursty load. Highlight that you proactively monitor **consumer lag**, have autoscaling policies for consumers, and build **circuit‑breaker behavior** to protect downstream systems from overload.

State that you prefer **immutable event logs** and **replayability**, enabling recovery and model re‑training for analytics. This links performance engineering to risk and governance.

### C) Producer Design (throughput + integrity)
- **Idempotent producer** to avoid duplicates.  
- **Acks=all** for durability, combined with sufficient ISR.  
- **Batching + compression** (lz4/snappy/zstd) to reduce network cost.  
- **Retries with jitter** and bounded retry policies.  
- **Transactions** only when you need exactly‑once semantics.

Producers are the first line of correctness. Call out the combination of **idempotent producers** + **acks=all** as your default for critical workflows. Explain why this avoids duplicate writes under retry and provides durability aligned with SLA/SLO targets.

Discuss **throughput tuning**: batch size, linger.ms, compression, and how you measure the trade‑off between latency and throughput. Show you understand that compression reduces network and disk usage at the cost of CPU, and that you tune based on bottlenecks.

For retries, emphasize **bounded retries with exponential backoff and jitter**, plus explicit error handling for non‑retriable conditions. This signals reliability thinking, not just “set retries high.”

Call out **transactional writes** only for cases that truly require exactly‑once semantics, because they introduce coordination overhead. You should position them as *business‑driven* choices, not technical defaults.

### D) Consumer Design (reliability + ordering)

### D) Consumer Design (reliability + ordering)
- **Consumer groups** for horizontal scale.  
- **At‑least‑once** delivery is default; design idempotent consumers.  
- **Exactly‑once** only if the business case warrants the complexity.  
- **Dead‑letter topics (DLQ)** for poison messages and replay strategy.  
- **Reprocessing**: keep immutable event logs for replay and audits.

Explain how you design consumers to be **idempotent** by default, because at‑least‑once delivery is the pragmatic trade‑off for scale. When duplicates are unacceptable, you use deduplication keys or stateful processing with checkpoints.

Ordering is scoped to partitions. Make clear that you don’t promise global order; you ensure business‑critical order (e.g., per account or trading book) and document the contract.

DLQs are a **safety valve**. Describe how you use DLQs to isolate poison messages, keep the pipeline flowing, and allow controlled replay once fixes are deployed. This is a key ops narrative.

Reprocessing is a first‑class capability, not a side effect. You retain event logs with clear retention policies so you can replay during incidents, audits, or model recalibration.

### E) Topic Strategy & Data Contracts
- **Topic per domain** or capability; avoid mega‑topics.  
- **Schema registry** (Avro/Protobuf) to enforce compatibility.  
- **Versioning** with backward‑compatible changes.  
- **Event time** vs. processing time; handle late/out‑of‑order events.

Your topic design should reflect business domains, not just teams. Explain that you avoid “mega‑topics” because they increase coupling and make governance harder. Instead, you create **domain‑aligned topics** with explicit contracts.

Schema registries enforce contract discipline. Make it clear you enforce **backward‑compatible schema changes**, and you include schema validation in CI/CD to prevent breaking changes.

Event time vs. processing time is a core concept. For trading, event time often reflects market data timestamps; processing time reflects system ingestion. You can explain how you handle **late events**, **out‑of‑order delivery**, and downstream aggregation.

### F) Observability & SRE Signals

### F) Observability & SRE Signals
- **Lag** (consumer offsets) is the first sign of risk.  
- **Broker health**: ISR count, under‑replicated partitions, request latency.  
- **Throughput**: produce/consume rates by topic and partition.  
- **Error budgets**: align with SLA/SLO.

Define the few metrics you always watch: consumer lag, under‑replicated partitions, broker request latency, and end‑to‑end processing time. Show you understand how each metric indicates a specific failure mode.

Tie SRE signals to business impact: lag = delayed decisions; under‑replication = durability risk; request latency = user experience and trading risk. This makes your answers executive‑ready.

Error budgets are the governance bridge. Explain how they justify system changes, risk tolerance, and when to slow delivery to stabilize platforms.

### G) Multi‑Region & DR Patterns
- **Active‑active vs. active‑passive**: choose based on latency and compliance.  
- **Replication:** MirrorMaker 2 / Cluster Linking for geo redundancy.  
- **Failover drills** with defined RTO/RPO.

In global trading, you must define **where order‑routing and pricing decisions happen**. Explain whether you use active‑active for latency and resilience, or active‑passive for strict control and compliance.

Replication is often a blend of **cluster linking** and **purpose‑built replication strategies** for regulatory boundaries. State that you validate replication with continuous tests and explicit failover playbooks.

Highlight that failover drills are not optional; they are **operational contracts**. The key is proving RTO/RPO in controlled exercises and retaining evidence for audit.

### H) Performance Checklist (fast recall)
- Partition count aligned to throughput and consumer parallelism.  
- Broker disk I/O sized for peak plus retention.  
- Compression enabled and tuned.  
- Batch sizes optimized for network + latency.  
- Clear retention and compaction policies.

Call out **hot partition** detection as a recurring performance issue at scale. Show how you rebalance keys or split topics to reduce contention.

Note that performance tuning is iterative: measure, tune, validate, and re‑baseline after every major topology change.

## 13) Why Kafka / Event‑Driven Systems (Interview Q&A)

**Q: Why did you choose Kafka instead of a traditional queue or synchronous API approach?**  
**A:** Kafka gives us **durable, replayable event logs** with high throughput and low latency, which is ideal for trading workflows where resilience, auditability, and reprocessing are mandatory. Traditional queues are great for point‑to‑point work, but they don’t provide the same event history, schema governance, or multi‑consumer scalability. We needed a platform that supports real‑time processing *and* post‑trade analysis at scale.

**Q: When is Kafka the wrong choice?**  
**A:** When ordering or delivery semantics must be strictly transactional across many services, or when the system is small enough that the overhead of Kafka isn’t justified. For small, synchronous workflows, a simpler queue or direct API is often better. Kafka is the right tool when event history, scale, and decoupling are business requirements.

**Q: How do you justify event‑driven architecture to executives?**  
**A:** It improves **resilience and scalability**, reduces coupling, and provides **full traceability**. That means faster recovery during incidents, better risk visibility, and the ability to onboard new analytics and AI capabilities without destabilizing core systems.

**Q: How do you handle ordering in Kafka?**  
**A:** Ordering is guaranteed within a partition, so we choose partition keys that reflect business ordering needs (e.g., account, portfolio, trading book). We avoid over‑constraining order globally, which would reduce throughput.

**Q: What are the biggest risks in Kafka at scale?**  
**A:** Hot partitions, consumer lag, under‑replicated partitions, and ungoverned schema changes. We manage these with proactive monitoring, autoscaling, schema registry enforcement, and disciplined topic design.

**Q: Why not use exactly‑once everywhere?**  
**A:** Exactly‑once introduces coordination overhead and complexity. We default to **at‑least‑once** with idempotent consumers, and reserve exactly‑once for the few flows where the business risk justifies it.
